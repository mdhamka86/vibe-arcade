# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

The primary user is Hammy — the one person who builds everything here and reaches
for it daily, mostly on a phone. The home page is a personal launcher first, but
it's a link that gets shared: friends and Lil Naddy land on it too, and a few of
the apps (Know Me, Tincture Time, Touch Grass) are built for Naddy specifically.
So the home speaks to an owner who knows every app by heart and to a first-time
visitor arriving cold, at the same time.

## Product Purpose

hammyLabs / vibe-arcade is a small house of vibe-coded browser experiments — games,
tools, and half-serious apps, several AI-powered, all shipped fast and for real life.
The home page (`public/index.html`) is the front door: a directory that both gets
Hammy into an app in one tap and stands as a proud shelf of the growing body of work.
Success is when opening it feels good — fast enough to be a daily launcher, considered
enough that sending someone the link is a small flex.

## Positioning

A one-person lab that ships real browser apps for its own life — the tools and games
someone actually uses, not a portfolio of concepts. The home is the shelf where that
body of work lives.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary action: open an app — dive into a game or reach for a tool. The tiles are
  the CTA; nothing should compete with the tap.
- Secondary action: browse and take in the collection — read the shelf, feel the range,
  come back later.
- The line a visitor remembers after ten seconds: *one person built all of this, and
  it has taste.*
- No formal proof, testimonials, or logos apply here — the proof is the work itself and
  the voice around it.

## Brand Personality

Warm and personal above all. This is a cozy workshop, not a company: the copy is witty
and self-deprecating ("no regard for sleep schedules," "Singaporean stubbornness"), it
signs off with a love note to Lil Naddy, and it leaves human fingerprints everywhere.
The voice is a maker talking about their own things — fond, a little wry, unpretentious.
Whatever the design does, it should read as made by a person who cares, for people they
care about.

## Anti-references

Not a dark, techy dashboard — no neon-on-black, no terminal-cliché coldness, no
clinical tool-shell. Not a generic SaaS or startup landing — no gradient hero, no
identical feature-card grid, no "sign up free" corporate polish. Not a cluttered app
store — no dense wall of samey tiles, ratings, and badges that overwhelms. The warmth
and the human hand are the whole point; anything that reads as impersonal or mass-made
has failed.

## Design Principles

Human fingerprints over polish. The personality — the asides, the love notes, the
voice — is not decoration on top of the product; it is the product. Keep it.

A proud shelf and a fast launcher at once. Beauty can't cost a tap. Every considered
touch has to survive the test of Hammy opening this on a phone and getting into an app
immediately.

Warm paper, not dark glass. When a choice could drift techy, cold, or clinical, pull
it back toward warm, tactile, and human. That reflex is the house style.

Curated, not catalogued. Resist the app-store wall. Give the collection room to breathe;
let each thing earn its place rather than packing the grid.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

This is a personal toy, and the stated priority is the vibe — no formal WCAG target and
no audit expected. That said, keep the baseline sane: legible body text with honest
contrast on the phone it's read on, and honor `prefers-reduced-motion`. Craft and
readability aren't at odds with the vibe; they're part of it.
